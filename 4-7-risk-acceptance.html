Risk Acceptance & Exception Management

When you can't patch, document it properly. The art of accepting risk responsibly.

What is Risk Acceptance?

Sometimes you CAN'T fix a vulnerability:
ğŸ­ Legacy system with no patch available
ğŸ’° Fix would cost more than the risk is worth
â° Business-critical system can't have downtime
ğŸ”§ Patch breaks critical functionality

Risk Acceptance = "We know about this risk, we've evaluated it, and we're choosing to accept it with proper documentation and approval."
ğŸ§  Risk Acceptance vs Exception - Know the Difference!
Risk AcceptancePermanent or long-term decision to live with the risk
ExceptionTemporary delay in remediation (has an end date)
False PositiveScanner was wrong - not actually vulnerable
Compensating ControlAlternative protection in place of the patch

Step 1: Document the Risk

ğŸ“‹ Required Documentation
  • Vulnerability Details: CVE, CVSS, affected systems
  • Business Justification: Why can't we patch?
  • Impact Analysis: What happens if exploited?
  • Compensating Controls: What mitigations are in place?
  • Residual Risk: What risk remains after mitigations?
  • Review Date: When will we reassess?

Step 2: Evaluate True Risk

Risk Evaluation Formula
Residual Risk = (Likelihood Ã— Impact) - Compensating Controls Effectiveness
Questions to Ask
  • Is this system internet-facing or internal only?
  • What data does it process? (PII, financial, public)
  • Is there active exploitation in the wild? (Check KEV)
  • What compensating controls exist?
  • What's the potential business impact?

Step 3: Justify the Decision

Think of it like insurance:

You're deciding whether to pay for full coverage or accept the risk of a smaller policy. You need to explain WHY you're making that choice and what you'll do if something goes wrong.

Example Justification

Vulnerability: CVE-2023-XXXX (CVSS 7.5) on legacy ERP system

Justification:
"The affected system runs a legacy ERP that's scheduled for decommission in Q2 2025. The vendor no longer provides patches. Applying the recommended workaround would break critical payroll functionality.

Mitigations in place:
- System isolated to dedicated VLAN
- Access restricted to 5 authorized users via jump host
- EDR monitoring with custom detection rule
- Daily backup with 4-hour RPO

Residual risk: LOW - system is not internet-facing, requires authenticated access, and has detection/recovery capabilities."

Step 4: Get Proper Approval

ğŸ§  Approval Authority by CVSS
Critical (9.0+)CISO or VP-level approval required
High (7.0-8.9)Security Director + Business Owner
Medium (4.0-6.9)Security Manager + System Owner
Low (0.1-3.9)Security Team Lead

Higher risk = Higher approval authority. The person approving OWNS the risk!

Interview Questions

ğŸ’¼ Interview Q&A

Q: "When is risk acceptance appropriate?"
"Risk acceptance is appropriate when remediation is impossible, impractical, or costs exceed the risk value. Examples include legacy systems with no patches, systems scheduled for decommission, or when patches break critical functionality. It must always include proper documentation, compensating controls, business owner approval, and a review date."

Q: "Who should approve risk acceptances?"
"The approval authority should match the risk level. Critical vulnerabilities need CISO-level approval. The key principle is that the BUSINESS owner - not IT - accepts the risk, because they're accountable for business outcomes."

Q: "How do you handle a risk acceptance that's been in place for 2 years?"
"I'd review it immediately. Risk acceptances should be reassessed at least annually. After 2 years, the threat landscape, system criticality, or business context may have changed. I'd verify the justification is still valid, compensating controls are still effective, and determine if new remediation options exist."
