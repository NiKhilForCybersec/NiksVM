Building a VM Program from Scratch

Establishing a vulnerability management program requires more than deploying a scanner. Successful programs are built on a foundation of executive sponsorship, clear policies, defined processes, and organizational buy-in. Here's a phased approach to building a mature VM program:

Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1-3)

  • Secure executive sponsorship and budget
  • Define program scope and objectives
  • Select and deploy scanning technology
  • Conduct initial asset discovery
  • Establish baseline vulnerability inventory
  • Identify key stakeholders across IT, DevOps, and business units

Phase 2: Operationalization (Months 3-6)

  • Develop and publish VM policy and SLAs
  • Define severity classification and prioritization criteria
  • Establish remediation workflows and ticketing integration
  • Implement regular scanning schedules
  • Create reporting templates for different audiences
  • Train asset owners on their responsibilities

Phase 3: Maturation (Months 6-12)

  • Integrate threat intelligence for risk-based prioritization
  • Automate routine tasks (scan scheduling, ticket creation, notifications)
  • Expand coverage to cloud, containers, and applications
  • Implement exception management process
  • Develop executive dashboards and trend reporting
  • Conduct program effectiveness reviews

Phase 4: Optimization (Ongoing)

  • Continuous process improvement based on metrics
  • Advanced automation with SOAR integration
  • Predictive analytics for vulnerability prioritization
  • Attack surface management integration
  • Continuous compliance monitoring
ğŸ’œ Program Launch Tip

Start with a pilot group of cooperative asset owners before organization-wide rollout. Use pilot results to refine processes, demonstrate quick wins, and build momentum for broader adoption.

Stakeholder Engagement

VM programs touch every part of the organization. Success depends on engaging stakeholders at all levels and translating security findings into language they understand.

Executive Leadership

Executives need to understand cyber risk in business terms. Focus on risk exposure, potential business impact, compliance implications, and return on security investment. Avoid technical jargon and vulnerability counts.

IT Operations

IT ops teams implement patches and configuration changes. They need clear priorities, realistic timelines, and assurance that security won't destabilize production systems. Build trust through proper testing processes and change management integration.

Development Teams

Developers need early feedback on application vulnerabilities. Integrate security scanning into CI/CD pipelines, provide remediation guidance, and work with them to prioritize findings that matter.

Business Unit Leaders

Business owners may not understand technical vulnerabilities but do understand risk to their operations. Translate findings into business impact and involve them in risk acceptance decisions.

Policy Development

A formal VM policy establishes the authority, scope, and requirements for the program. The policy should be approved by executive leadership and communicated organization-wide.

Policy Components

Purpose and Scope

Define why the policy exists and what assets/systems it covers. Specify any exclusions and the process for requesting exceptions.

Roles and Responsibilities

Clearly define who is responsible for scanning, analysis, remediation, verification, and oversight. Include escalation paths for non-compliance.

Scanning Requirements

Specify minimum scan frequencies by asset type, authenticated vs. unauthenticated scanning requirements, and scan window restrictions.

Remediation SLAs

Define maximum remediation timelines by severity level. Include provisions for emergency patching and risk acceptance procedures.

Exception Process

Document how exceptions are requested, reviewed, approved, and tracked. Include required compensating controls and maximum exception durations.

SLA Definition

Service Level Agreements define the expected remediation timelines and create accountability. SLAs should be realistic, risk-based, and aligned with business needs.

Factors Influencing SLAs

  • Vulnerability Severity: Critical findings require faster response
  • Asset Exposure: Internet-facing systems need shorter SLAs
  • Exploit Availability: Weaponized exploits demand urgent action
  • Business Criticality: Crown jewels vs. non-critical systems
  • Regulatory Requirements: PCI DSS, HIPAA may mandate specific timelines

RACI Matrix

A RACI matrix clarifies roles and responsibilities for each VM process activity. RACI stands for Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed.

  • Responsible (R): Person who performs the work
  • Accountable (A): Person ultimately answerable for completion
  • Consulted (C): Person whose input is sought before action
  • Informed (I): Person kept updated on progress

VM Maturity Model

Organizations progress through maturity levels as their VM program evolves. Understanding your current maturity helps identify improvement areas and set realistic goals.

Level 1: Reactive

Ad-hoc vulnerability management with no formal program. Scanning occurs sporadically or only after incidents. No defined SLAs, metrics, or ownership. Remediation is inconsistent and untracked.

Level 2: Managed

Basic scanning program established with regular scan schedules. Vulnerabilities are tracked in spreadsheets or basic ticketing. SLAs exist but compliance is inconsistent. Limited automation and reporting.

Level 3: Defined

Formal VM policy and procedures documented. Enterprise scanning platform deployed with authenticated scanning. Ticketing integration for remediation tracking. Regular metrics and executive reporting. Exception process established.

Level 4: Proactive

Risk-based prioritization using threat intelligence and asset criticality. Automated workflows for ticket creation and notification. Strong SLA compliance. Integration with SIEM and incident response. Continuous monitoring and trending.

Level 5: Optimized

Predictive analytics and machine learning for prioritization. Full automation with SOAR integration. Attack surface management and exposure assessment. Continuous compliance monitoring. Industry benchmarking and continuous improvement.

Common Program Pitfalls

Learn from common mistakes that derail VM programs:

  • Tool-First Thinking: Deploying a scanner without process and governance leads to scanner shelfware
  • Lack of Executive Support: Without leadership backing, remediation teams won't prioritize security
  • Unrealistic SLAs: Timelines teams can't meet lead to exceptions, gaming, and credibility loss
  • No Asset Ownership: Orphaned vulnerabilities with no clear owner never get fixed
  • Metric Obsession: Focusing on vulnerability counts rather than risk reduction
  • Ignoring Business Context: Treating all assets equally regardless of criticality
  • Manual Everything: Relying on manual processes that don't scale

Build relationships before you need them. Engage IT ops, DevOps, and business leaders during program developmentâ€”not just when vulnerabilities need fixing. Partners who understand the program are more likely to support remediation efforts.
